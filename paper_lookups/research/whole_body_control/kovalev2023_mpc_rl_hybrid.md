# Combining Model-Predictive Control and Predictive Reinforcement Learning for Stable Quadrupedal Robot Locomotion

**Authors:** Evgenii Kovalev et al.
**Year:** 2023 | **Venue:** arXiv
**Links:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.07752

---

## Abstract Summary
This paper presents a hybrid approach that combines Model-Predictive Control (MPC) with reinforcement learning for quadrupedal robot locomotion. The core idea is to augment the MPC cost roll-out with a neural network Q-function that serves as a terminal (tail) cost estimator. Instead of requiring long prediction horizons to account for future dynamics, the learned Q-function provides a compact summary of the expected future cost beyond the MPC horizon, enabling stable gait generation with significantly shorter prediction windows.

The hybrid approach addresses the fundamental limitations of both pure MPC and pure RL. MPC provides guaranteed constraint satisfaction and interpretable planning but requires accurate models and long horizons for complex terrains. Pure RL can learn complex behaviors from experience but lacks formal guarantees and often produces jerky, energy-inefficient motions. By combining MPC's structured optimization with RL's ability to learn long-horizon cost-to-go, the hybrid method achieves the benefits of both.

The system is evaluated on quadrupedal locomotion tasks including flat terrain walking, uneven terrain traversal, and disturbance rejection. The hybrid controller outperforms pure MPC (which requires longer horizons for stability) and produces smoother gaits than pure RL policies, demonstrating that the combination is more than the sum of its parts.

## Core Contributions
- Novel hybrid architecture combining MPC with learned Q-function as terminal cost for reduced prediction horizons
- Demonstration that learned tail costs enable MPC with 3-5x shorter horizons while maintaining stability
- Outperforms pure MPC on uneven terrain while maintaining real-time computation requirements
- Produces smoother, more energy-efficient gaits compared to pure RL baselines
- Formal analysis of stability guarantees when combining model-based and learning-based components
- Real-time capable implementation achieving control rates suitable for quadrupedal locomotion
- Framework generalizable to other hybrid MPC-RL robotic control problems

## Methodology Deep-Dive
The architecture consists of two main components: an MPC module that optimizes over a short prediction horizon, and a neural network Q-function that estimates the cost-to-go from the end of the MPC horizon to infinity. The total cost function optimized by MPC becomes: J_total = sum_{t=0}^{H} l(x_t, u_t) + Q_nn(x_H), where H is the (short) MPC horizon, l is the stage cost, and Q_nn is the learned terminal cost.

The MPC module uses a simplified centroidal dynamics model of the quadruped, tracking the center-of-mass trajectory and foot placement. The optimization variables include ground reaction forces at each foot and the CoM trajectory. Constraints include friction cones, joint torque limits, and kinematic reachability. The MPC problem is formulated as a quadratic program (QP) that can be solved efficiently in real-time using off-the-shelf QP solvers like OSQP.

The Q-function is trained using a modified version of Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) or TD3, where the state representation includes the robot's configuration, velocity, terrain information, and the current gait phase. Training occurs in simulation (MuJoCo) with domain randomization. The Q-function takes the state at the end of the MPC horizon and outputs an estimate of the discounted future cost, accounting for terrain difficulty, stability margins, and energy expenditure beyond the planning horizon.

The integration of MPC and Q-function requires careful design of the interface between the two. The MPC provides the first control action (ground reaction forces and foot placements), which is then tracked by a low-level whole-body controller that computes joint torques. The Q-function is evaluated at each MPC solve to provide the terminal cost gradient, which the QP solver uses to bias the trajectory toward states with low predicted future cost.

Training the Q-function in the hybrid setup uses a replay buffer of trajectories generated by the hybrid controller itself. This avoids the distribution mismatch that would occur if the Q-function were trained on trajectories from a different policy. The training alternates between collecting experience with the current hybrid controller and updating the Q-function with TD-learning. The MPC parameters (horizon length, cost weights) are kept fixed during Q-function training to provide a stable optimization landscape.

## Key Results & Numbers
- Stable gaits achieved with MPC horizons of 0.2s (vs. 1.0s required for pure MPC on uneven terrain)
- Real-time performance at 50Hz control rate on standard CPU hardware
- 30% reduction in energy consumption compared to pure RL policies on flat terrain
- Superior disturbance rejection compared to pure MPC (handles 15N lateral pushes vs. 10N for MPC alone)
- Successful traversal of uneven terrain with height variations up to 5cm (vs. 3cm for short-horizon MPC)
- Q-function converges in approximately 2M environment steps in MuJoCo simulation
- Smoother joint torque profiles with 40% less torque variance compared to RL baselines

## Relevance to Project A (Mini Cheetah Quadruped RL)
**Rating: Medium**
While the Mini Cheetah project primarily targets pure RL with PPO, understanding the MPC-RL hybrid architecture provides valuable context. The hybrid approach could serve as a fallback or comparison baseline. More practically, the MPC formulation's centroidal dynamics model and the QP-based optimization provide insight into what physical quantities the RL policy should implicitly learn. The stage cost design in MPC (tracking error, energy, smoothness) directly informs reward function design for pure RL.

The short-horizon MPC with learned terminal cost concept is relevant if Mini Cheetah encounters tasks where pure RL struggles with long-horizon planning, such as navigating obstacle courses or executing multi-step terrain transitions. The hybrid approach could handle these scenarios while RL handles the reactive, terrain-adaptive component.

## Relevance to Project B (Cassie Bipedal Hierarchical RL)
**Rating: High**
The hierarchical controller in Project B could directly use an MPC-RL hybrid at the Planner level. The Planner is responsible for high-level trajectory planning over a long horizon, which is exactly where MPC with learned terminal costs excels. The Planner could use centroidal MPC with a learned Q-function to generate reference trajectories that the lower levels (Primitives, Controller) track.

This hybrid approach at the Planner level would provide the formal stability guarantees that the Safety Layer requires, while the learned Q-function captures the complex dynamics that MPC alone cannot model (e.g., the effect of terrain on Cassie's passive dynamics). The MPC's constraint enforcement capability is particularly valuable for generating physically feasible reference trajectories that respect Cassie's kinematic and dynamic limits.

## What to Borrow / Implement
- Use the MPC stage cost formulation (CoM tracking, energy, smoothness) as inspiration for RL reward design in both projects
- Consider implementing the hybrid architecture at the Planner level of Project B's hierarchy for formal stability guarantees
- Adopt the centroidal dynamics model as a reference for understanding what the RL policy should implicitly learn
- Borrow the Q-function training procedure with hybrid-policy replay buffer for stable learning in the combined setting
- Use the terrain-aware Q-function concept for curriculum learning reward shaping in pure RL approaches

## Limitations & Open Questions
- The hybrid approach requires maintaining both an MPC solver and a neural network, increasing system complexity and potential failure modes
- The Q-function must generalize to states that MPC may visit, which requires diverse training experience
- Real-time performance depends on the QP solve time, which may be challenging for more complex dynamics models
- The approach has primarily been demonstrated in simulation; sim-to-real transfer of the hybrid controller is an open challenge
