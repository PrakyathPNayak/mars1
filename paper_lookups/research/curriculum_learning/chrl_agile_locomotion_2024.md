# Learning Agility and Adaptive Legged Locomotion via Curricular Hindsight Reinforcement Learning

**Authors:** (Nature Scientific Reports 2024)
**Year:** 2024 | **Venue:** Nature Scientific Reports
**Links:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-79292-4

---

## Abstract Summary
This paper introduces Curricular Hindsight Reinforcement Learning (CHRL), a training framework that combines automatic curriculum learning with Hindsight Experience Replay (HER) to train agile quadruped locomotion policies. CHRL addresses two fundamental challenges in legged locomotion RL: (1) the difficulty of learning from sparse or distant rewards in complex locomotion tasks, and (2) the need for progressive difficulty scaling without manual curriculum design. By integrating HER's ability to learn from failed trajectories with an automatic curriculum that adapts difficulty based on the agent's current competence, CHRL enables a quadruped to master high-speed running, fall recovery, and rapid turning on challenging outdoor terrains.

The framework operates on the principle that every trajectory—even failures—contains useful information. When the robot attempts a high-speed turn and falls, HER relabels the trajectory with an achievable goal (e.g., the actual turning radius achieved), providing a learning signal. Simultaneously, the automatic curriculum adjusts the distribution of commanded goals to maintain an optimal difficulty level, staying in the zone of proximal development where the agent is challenged but not overwhelmed.

The combined CHRL approach demonstrates substantial improvements over both standalone curriculum learning and standalone HER, achieving peak forward velocity of 3.45 m/s, rapid 180° turning in under 1.5 seconds, and successful fall recovery from lateral pushes on unstructured outdoor terrains. The policy generalizes across surfaces including grass, gravel, and asphalt without terrain-specific training.

## Core Contributions
- CHRL framework combining automatic curriculum learning with Hindsight Experience Replay for legged locomotion, addressing sparse reward and difficulty scaling simultaneously
- Automatic curriculum based on agent competence estimation using a success-rate moving average, adjusting goal difficulty without manual threshold design
- HER integration for goal-conditioned locomotion where failed attempts (e.g., commanded 3 m/s but achieved 2 m/s) generate valid training data by relabeling with achieved goals
- Demonstration of high-speed locomotion (3.45 m/s), rapid turning (180° in <1.5 s), and fall recovery on outdoor terrains using a single unified policy
- Comprehensive ablation showing CHRL outperforms curriculum-only (+35% success rate) and HER-only (+28% success rate) on the hardest locomotion tasks
- Cross-terrain generalization without terrain-specific training, validated on grass, gravel, asphalt, and mild slopes
- Training efficiency: CHRL achieves expert-level performance in 60% of the training iterations required by vanilla PPO

## Methodology Deep-Dive
CHRL formulates locomotion as a goal-conditioned RL problem where the goal g specifies desired velocity (v_x, v_y, ω_z). The policy π(a|s,g) maps proprioceptive state s and goal g to joint position actions. The reward is an exponential kernel on goal achievement: r = exp(-||v_achieved - g||² / σ²), providing a smooth, bounded signal that is sparse for difficult goals and dense for achievable ones.

The automatic curriculum maintains a distribution over goal difficulties parameterized by the maximum goal magnitude. A competence estimator tracks the exponential moving average of episode success rates (defined as achieving >80% of the commanded velocity for >70% of the episode). When the success rate exceeds a threshold θ_high = 0.7, the curriculum advances (increases maximum goal difficulty by 10%); when it drops below θ_low = 0.3, the curriculum retreats (decreases by 5%). This asymmetric advance/retreat ensures stable progression.

HER is integrated with the "future" strategy: for each trajectory, k=4 additional training samples are generated by replacing the original goal with goals achieved at future timesteps in the same trajectory. For locomotion, this means if the robot was commanded to run at 3 m/s but only achieved 2.1 m/s, HER creates training samples with the achieved velocity as the goal, providing positive reward signals for what the robot actually accomplished. This dramatically improves sample efficiency for difficult goals where successes are rare.

The observation space includes joint positions (12), joint velocities (12), base angular velocity (3), projected gravity (3), previous action (12), and the goal velocity command (3). The action space outputs 12 target joint positions at 50 Hz. The policy and value networks are separate MLPs (256×256) with ReLU activations. Training uses PPO with clip ε=0.2, γ=0.99, λ_GAE=0.95, learning rate 3×10⁻⁴, minibatch size 4096.

Domain randomization includes friction (0.2–1.5), payload (0–5 kg), motor strength (±15%), joint damping (±20%), base mass (±10%), and push perturbations (random forces up to 80 N applied every 5–10 seconds during training). Terrain randomization uses a procedurally generated heightfield with varying roughness levels from smooth to ±5 cm irregularities. Training runs for 10,000 iterations with 4096 parallel environments in Isaac Gym, taking approximately 16 hours on an NVIDIA A100 GPU.

## Key Results & Numbers
- Maximum forward velocity: 3.45 m/s (approximately 7× body length per second)
- 180° turning time: <1.5 seconds at 1.0 m/s forward velocity
- Fall recovery success rate: 87% from lateral pushes of 80 N
- CHRL vs. curriculum-only: +35% success rate on goals above 2.5 m/s
- CHRL vs. HER-only: +28% success rate on goals above 2.5 m/s
- CHRL vs. vanilla PPO: 60% fewer training iterations to reach equivalent performance
- Cross-terrain generalization: <8% performance degradation across grass, gravel, and asphalt
- Training time: ~16 hours on single A100 with 4096 environments
- Velocity tracking RMSE: 0.12 m/s at moderate speeds, 0.25 m/s at maximum speed

## Relevance to Project A (Mini Cheetah Quadruped RL)
**Rating: High**
CHRL is critically relevant to Mini Cheetah's curriculum design. The automatic curriculum component eliminates the need for hand-crafting velocity and terrain difficulty schedules, which is a significant engineering burden in current sim-to-real pipelines. The HER integration is particularly valuable for Mini Cheetah's high-speed locomotion training, where early policies frequently fail to achieve commanded velocities—HER ensures these failure trajectories contribute to learning rather than being wasted.

The demonstrated performance metrics (3.45 m/s, rapid turning, fall recovery) align well with Mini Cheetah's target capabilities. The automatic curriculum's competence-based progression rate would replace Mini Cheetah's current manual curriculum stages, adapting to the specific learning dynamics of each training run. The push perturbation training (up to 80 N) directly translates to Mini Cheetah's robustness requirements.

## Relevance to Project B (Cassie Bipedal Hierarchical RL)
**Rating: High**
CHRL's curriculum strategy is directly applicable to Cassie's adversarial curriculum training at the Primitives and Controller levels. The automatic competence-based difficulty scaling could replace manually-designed adversarial schedules, allowing each level of Cassie's hierarchy to progress at its own rate. HER is particularly relevant for Cassie's Primitives level, where DIAYN/DADS skill discovery could benefit from relabeling failed skill executions with actually-achieved behaviors.

The goal-conditioned formulation naturally maps to Cassie's Planner→Primitives interface, where high-level plans specify goal states that the Primitives level must achieve. CHRL would improve the Primitives level's ability to learn from trajectories where commanded behaviors were only partially achieved.

## What to Borrow / Implement
- Implement CHRL's automatic curriculum with competence-based progression for Mini Cheetah's velocity and terrain difficulty scheduling
- Integrate HER with the "future" strategy into Mini Cheetah's PPO pipeline for goal-conditioned velocity tracking
- Adopt the asymmetric advance/retreat curriculum dynamics (10% advance, 5% retreat) for stable training progression
- Apply CHRL's curriculum framework to Cassie's Primitives-level training with DIAYN/DADS goal relabeling
- Use the push perturbation training protocol (random 80 N pushes every 5–10 s) for robustness training on both platforms

## Limitations & Open Questions
- HER's "future" strategy assumes that achieved states at future timesteps represent valid alternative goals; for locomotion, this is mostly true for velocity goals but may not hold for complex terrain-navigation goals
- The automatic curriculum uses simple success-rate thresholds; more sophisticated competence models (e.g., Bayesian progress estimation) could improve curriculum efficiency
- The 3.45 m/s maximum speed is impressive but below Mini Cheetah's theoretical capability (~3.7 m/s); the curriculum may plateau before reaching the robot's physical limits
- Integration of CHRL with hierarchical architectures (relevant for Cassie) is not explored; curriculum and HER interactions across hierarchy levels remain an open research question
