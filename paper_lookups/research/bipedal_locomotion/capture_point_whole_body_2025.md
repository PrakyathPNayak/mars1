# Whole-Body Motion Generation for Balancing of Biped Robot Using Capture Point and MPC

**Authors:** (MDPI Applied Sciences 2025)
**Year:** 2025 | **Venue:** MDPI Applied Sciences
**Links:** https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/15/11/5828

## Abstract Summary
This paper presents a whole-body motion generation framework for bipedal robot balancing that combines capture point theory with model-predictive control (MPC). The central contribution is using the capture point to correct the Zero Moment Point (ZMP) in real-time, ensuring dynamic stability during walking and under external disturbances. The corrected ZMP reference is then tracked by an MPC-based controller that generates full-body motions including leg, torso, and arm trajectories.

A distinguishing feature of this work is the integration of arm motion for momentum management. When the robot experiences perturbations (pushes, uneven terrain), the arms generate angular momentum to assist balance recovery, similar to how humans swing their arms during walking or when stumbling. The whole-body MPC optimizes over the full kinematic chain, coordinating leg stepping, torso posture, and arm swing to achieve natural gait patterns and robust disturbance rejection.

The system achieves natural walking gaits, robust push recovery (withstanding pushes that cause significant ZMP displacement), and effective balance maintenance on uneven surfaces. The capture point-based ZMP correction provides a principled stability criterion that the MPC uses as a constraint, ensuring that all generated motions are dynamically feasible and balance-maintaining. The framework operates in real-time, making it suitable for deployment on physical bipedal robots.

## Core Contributions
- Integration of capture point theory with MPC for principled dynamic balance in bipedal robots
- Capture point-based ZMP correction that provides real-time stability guarantees
- Whole-body MPC that coordinates legs, torso, and arms for natural motion generation
- Arm momentum management for enhanced push recovery and disturbance rejection
- Natural walking gait generation with human-like arm swing and weight transfer
- Real-time capable implementation suitable for hardware deployment
- Comprehensive evaluation including push recovery, uneven terrain, and gait transitions

## Methodology Deep-Dive
The framework operates on a three-layer architecture. The top layer computes the desired capture point trajectory based on the walking plan (foot placement sequence, timing, desired velocity). The middle layer uses capture point feedback to correct the ZMP reference and feeds it to an MPC that generates CoM and full-body motion trajectories. The bottom layer tracks these trajectories with a whole-body inverse dynamics controller that computes joint torques.

The capture point dynamics follow the standard LIPM-based formulation: xi_dot = omega * (xi - r_zmp), where xi is the capture point position, omega is the natural frequency sqrt(g/z_com), and r_zmp is the ZMP position. For stable walking, the capture point must be controlled to converge toward a desired trajectory. The control law corrects the ZMP reference: r_zmp_corrected = r_zmp_nominal + K_cp * (xi - xi_desired), where K_cp is a gain matrix that determines the aggressiveness of balance correction.

The MPC formulation uses a preview control approach with a horizon of 1-2 seconds. The optimization variables include the CoM jerk trajectory (third derivative of CoM position), from which CoM position, velocity, and acceleration are reconstructed through integration. The cost function minimizes tracking error of the corrected ZMP reference while penalizing excessive CoM motion: J = sum_t [w_zmp * ||zmp_t - r_zmp_corrected||^2 + w_jerk * ||j_t||^2]. The ZMP is computed from the CoM trajectory using the standard relationship: zmp = com - (z_com / g) * com_ddot.

The whole-body extension incorporates arm motion by including the angular momentum generated by arm swing in the ZMP computation. The modified ZMP equation becomes: zmp = com - (z_com / g) * com_ddot - (1 / (m * g)) * dL/dt, where L is the angular momentum contributed by the arms. The MPC optimizes arm trajectories to generate beneficial angular momentum during perturbation recovery while minimizing arm motion during steady-state walking. This allows the arms to act as active balance aids without unnecessarily large motions during normal locomotion.

The whole-body inverse dynamics controller at the lowest level converts the desired CoM trajectory, foot trajectories, and arm trajectories into joint torques. This uses a QP-based approach that minimizes torque while satisfying the dynamics equations, contact constraints (friction cones, unilateral contact forces), and joint limits. The controller operates at a higher frequency (1kHz) than the MPC (100-200Hz) to ensure accurate trajectory tracking.

## Key Results & Numbers
- Push recovery: withstands lateral pushes up to 100N applied for 0.1s (impulse of 10 Ns)
- Walking speed: stable walking at velocities from 0.2 to 1.2 m/s
- Arm contribution: 30% improvement in push recovery magnitude when arms are active vs. locked
- ZMP tracking error: less than 2cm RMS during steady-state walking
- Capture point convergence: disturbance recovery within 2-3 steps
- Real-time performance: MPC solve time under 5ms at 200Hz update rate
- Uneven terrain: stable walking on surfaces with up to 3cm height variation

## Relevance to Project A (Mini Cheetah Quadruped RL)
**Rating: Low**
This paper is specific to bipedal balance and whole-body control, which has limited direct applicability to quadruped locomotion. The capture point concept is fundamentally a bipedal stability criterion. However, the MPC formulation and whole-body coordination principles provide general insights for robot control architecture design. The multi-rate control hierarchy (MPC at 200Hz, inverse dynamics at 1kHz) is a common pattern in legged locomotion that also applies to quadrupeds.

## Relevance to Project B (Cassie Bipedal Hierarchical RL)
**Rating: Critical**
Project B uses Differentiable Capture Point as a key component in the CPTE (Capture Point Trajectory Estimator) module. This paper provides the complete theoretical and practical framework for capture point-based balance control that CPTE builds upon. The capture point dynamics equations, ZMP correction law, and MPC integration directly inform the design of Project B's Controller level.

The three-layer architecture (capture point planning, MPC trajectory generation, inverse dynamics tracking) maps closely to Project B's hierarchy: the Planner level corresponds to capture point trajectory planning, the Controller level performs trajectory optimization (analogous to the MPC layer), and the Safety Layer enforces constraints (analogous to the inverse dynamics layer with contact constraints). The arm momentum management concept could be extended to Cassie's arm-like components or adapted for momentum management through hip and torso motion.

The capture point-based ZMP correction law (r_zmp_corrected = r_zmp_nominal + K_cp * (xi - xi_desired)) could be directly implemented in the CPTE module as a differentiable operation, providing gradient information for end-to-end training. The gain K_cp could be learned rather than fixed, allowing the system to adapt its balance recovery aggressiveness based on the situation.

## What to Borrow / Implement

- Use the MPC formulation (preview control with CoM jerk optimization) as a reference for the Controller level's trajectory generation
- Adopt the angular momentum management concept for Cassie's upper body to improve push recovery
- Borrow the multi-rate control hierarchy design (200Hz planning, 1kHz tracking) for Project B's control architecture
- Implement the capture point convergence criterion as a stability metric for the Safety Layer's LCBF constraints

## Limitations & Open Questions
- The LIPM-based capture point formulation assumes constant CoM height, limiting applicability to dynamic maneuvers like jumping or crouching
- Arm motion optimization adds computational overhead that may be challenging for more complex whole-body models
- The MPC's linear model (constant CoM height) may not accurately predict dynamics during large perturbations
- Integration with learning-based components (RL policies) for the whole-body controller is not explored; combining this framework with learned policies is an open research direction
