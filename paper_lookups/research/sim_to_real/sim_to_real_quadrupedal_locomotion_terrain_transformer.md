**Title:** Sim-to-Real Transfer for Quadrupedal Locomotion via Terrain Transformer
**Authors:** Hang Lai, Weinan Zhang, Xialin He, Yu Zheng, Yong Yu
**Year:** 2023
**Venue:** ICRA 2023 (IEEE International Conference on Robotics and Automation)
**arXiv / DOI:** arXiv:2212.07740

**Abstract Summary (2–3 sentences):**
The Terrain Transformer (TERT) applies transformer-based sequence modeling to quadrupedal locomotion, using a two-stage training framework with offline pretraining and online correction to bridge the sim-to-real gap. The method leverages privileged terrain information during training and demonstrates superior performance over baselines on challenging terrains including sandpits, stairs, and uneven ground in real-world deployment.

**Core Contributions (bullet list, 4–7 items):**
- First application of the transformer architecture to quadrupedal locomotion control
- Two-stage framework: offline pretraining on privileged data + online RL fine-tuning
- Causal self-attention mechanism for processing proprioceptive history sequences
- Effective encoding of privileged terrain information during the pretraining phase
- Real-world validation on physical quadruped over sandpits, stairs, and rough terrain
- Outperforms TCN-based and MLP-based baselines on challenging terrain navigation
- Demonstrates that transformer attention can capture long-range temporal dependencies in locomotion

**Methodology Deep-Dive (3–5 paragraphs):**
TERT introduces a transformer-based policy architecture for quadrupedal locomotion. The key insight is that locomotion control benefits from modeling long-range temporal dependencies in proprioceptive sequences — for example, detecting periodic gait patterns, anticipating terrain changes based on historical contact patterns, and maintaining stability through consistent temporal reasoning. Standard MLPs and even TCNs have limited capacity for such long-range modeling.

The training proceeds in two stages. Stage 1 (offline pretraining): a transformer policy is pretrained using behavioral cloning on trajectories generated by a privileged teacher policy that has access to ground-truth terrain height maps. The transformer uses causal self-attention to process sequences of (observation, action) pairs, learning to predict optimal actions conditioned on the full history of interactions. This pretraining provides a strong initialization that captures terrain-adaptive behaviors.

Stage 2 (online correction): the pretrained transformer policy is fine-tuned using PPO in simulation. This online phase corrects any distributional shift from the offline data and optimizes the policy for the RL reward function. The combined approach leverages the data efficiency of offline pretraining with the optimality of online RL.

The transformer architecture uses 4 layers with 8 attention heads and a context length covering the most recent 50 timesteps. Positional encodings enable the model to reason about temporal ordering. Domain randomization during Stage 2 ensures robust sim-to-real transfer by varying terrain, dynamics, and sensor noise.

**Key Results & Numbers:**
- Outperformed TCN-based baselines by 15–20% in terrain traversal success rate
- Successful real-world deployment on stairs (20 cm height), sandpits, and unstructured ground
- Training convergence ~30% faster than pure RL baselines due to offline pretraining
- Attention analysis reveals the model learns to focus on recent contacts and periodic patterns
- Real-world velocity tracking error < 0.15 m/s at commanded speeds up to 1.0 m/s

**Relevance to Project A (Mini Cheetah):** HIGH — The transformer-based locomotion architecture and two-stage training framework are directly applicable to improving the Mini Cheetah's policy capacity and training efficiency.
**Relevance to Project B (Cassie HRL):** HIGH — Directly relevant to the Dual Asymmetric-Context Transformer component; the attention-based temporal reasoning aligns with the project's transformer architecture goals.

**What to Borrow / Implement:**
- Explore replacing the MLP policy with a transformer-based architecture for improved temporal reasoning
- Adopt the two-stage (pretraining + online RL) training pipeline for faster convergence
- Use attention visualization to interpret what the policy learns about terrain and gait dynamics

**Limitations & Open Questions:**
- Transformer policies have higher inference latency (~3x) compared to MLP policies, potentially limiting control frequency
- The offline pretraining stage requires a well-performing teacher policy, creating a bootstrap dependency
- Context length is a critical hyperparameter — too short loses terrain information, too long increases compute
