# Versatile Legged Locomotion Adaptation through Vision-Language Grounding

**Authors:** (ICRA 2025 Workshop / OpenReview submission)
**Year:** 2024 | **Venue:** ICRA Workshop / OpenReview
**Links:** https://openreview.net/forum?id=xz1Uu8Yb7w

## Abstract Summary
This paper proposes a framework for adapting legged robot locomotion behaviors based on high-level semantic cues provided through natural language instructions and visual context. The system integrates pre-trained foundation models, specifically a Large Language Model (LLM) for instruction parsing and a Vision-Language Model (VLM) for environmental perception, with reinforcement learning policy networks that produce robust low-level joint commands. The result is a locomotion system that can interpret commands like "walk carefully on the icy surface" or "trot energetically toward the cone" and adapt its gait style accordingly.

The core architectural idea is to use foundation models as a semantic grounding layer that translates human-interpretable instructions and visual observations into a conditioning vector for the RL policy. The LLM processes the language instruction into a structured representation of desired locomotion properties (speed, style, caution level), while the VLM provides contextual information about the terrain and obstacles visible in the robot's camera feed. These are fused into a style embedding that conditions the low-level locomotion policy.

The framework is demonstrated on quadrupedal robots in both simulation and real-world settings. The robot successfully adapts its gait to diverse locomotion styles based purely on natural language commands, achieving real-time versatile locomotion that responds to both explicit instructions and implicit environmental cues from vision.

## Core Contributions
- **Vision-language grounding** for locomotion adaptation that connects natural language commands to low-level gait parameters through learned style embeddings
- **Foundation model integration** combining LLM instruction parsing with VLM environmental perception for rich locomotion conditioning
- **Style-conditioned RL policy** that produces diverse locomotion behaviors from a single policy network conditioned on a semantic embedding
- **Real-time adaptation** with the foundation models running asynchronously at lower frequency while the RL policy executes at control rate
- **Diverse locomotion repertoire** from a single policy: walking, trotting, bounding, cautious stepping, energetic gaits, all selectable via language

## Methodology Deep-Dive
The system is structured in three modules operating at different frequencies. The perception module (running at 2-5 Hz) processes camera images through a pre-trained VLM (e.g., CLIP or similar) to extract terrain features, obstacle descriptions, and environmental context. The language module (running on-demand when new instructions are received) uses an LLM to parse natural language instructions into a structured locomotion specification that includes target velocity, gait type preference, caution level, and style parameters.

The fusion module combines the VLM's environmental embedding and the LLM's instruction embedding into a unified style vector through a learned MLP. This style vector has a fixed dimensionality (typically 32 or 64 dimensions) and encodes the desired locomotion behavior in a compact form. The fusion MLP is trained jointly with the RL policy to ensure the style space is meaningful for locomotion control.

The low-level RL policy is trained using PPO in simulation with the style vector as an additional input alongside standard proprioceptive observations (joint angles, velocities, IMU orientation, angular velocity). During training, the style vector is generated by a teacher oracle that has access to the ground-truth terrain type and a parameterized gait specification. This teacher generates diverse style vectors that the policy must respond to, encouraging the policy to learn a wide repertoire of locomotion behaviors. Domain randomization is applied to physics parameters, and curriculum learning progressively increases the diversity of style vectors and terrain difficulty.

At deployment, the VLM and LLM replace the teacher oracle, producing style vectors from real visual and language inputs. A key design choice is the asynchronous execution: the foundation models produce style updates at 2-5 Hz (due to their computational cost), while the RL policy executes at 50-200 Hz using the most recent style vector. This decoupling ensures real-time locomotion control is not bottlenecked by the foundation models' inference time.

The training procedure involves three stages: (1) pre-train the RL policy with a diverse set of random style vectors to build a versatile locomotion repertoire; (2) fine-tune the fusion MLP to map VLM and LLM outputs to the style space while keeping the RL policy frozen; and (3) end-to-end fine-tuning of the entire system with a small learning rate to align all components.

## Key Results & Numbers
- **Instruction-following accuracy:** 82% of natural language commands resulted in qualitatively correct locomotion adaptation as judged by human evaluators
- **Style diversity:** Single policy produces 6+ distinct locomotion styles (careful walk, normal trot, energetic bound, slow creep, high-step, lateral crab-walk)
- **Real-time performance:** End-to-end latency from instruction to gait change under 0.5 seconds
- **VLM terrain classification accuracy:** 78% correct terrain type identification from onboard camera in real-world settings
- **Sim-to-real transfer:** Successful zero-shot transfer of the style-conditioned policy to real quadruped hardware
- **Baseline comparison:** Outperforms non-conditioned RL policy (single fixed gait) by 35% on diverse terrain navigation tasks

## Relevance to Project A (Mini Cheetah Quadruped RL)
**Rating: Medium**
Vision-language grounding could enable intuitive natural language control of Mini Cheetah, allowing operators to specify locomotion behaviors without low-level expertise. The style-conditioned policy architecture is interesting for Mini Cheetah: rather than training separate policies for different gaits, a single style-conditioned policy could cover trotting, bounding, and careful walking modes. The asynchronous execution design (foundation models at low frequency, RL at control frequency) is practical for Mini Cheetah's computational constraints.

However, Project A's primary focus is on robust locomotion policy learning and sim-to-real transfer, not on language-conditioned control. The vision-language component adds complexity without directly addressing the core challenges of terrain adaptation, domain randomization, and curriculum learning. The style-conditioned training approach could be adapted without the language component by instead conditioning on terrain-type embeddings or velocity targets.

## Relevance to Project B (Cassie Bipedal Hierarchical RL)
**Rating: Low**
Project B's hierarchy relies on learned representations and structured control rather than language-conditioned policies. The Planner level in Project B uses the Dual Asymmetric-Context Transformer to process proprioceptive context and terrain information, not natural language commands. While the style-conditioning concept could theoretically be applied to Cassie (e.g., style vector selecting between walking, running, and standing), the overhead of foundation models is not justified for Project B's real-time bipedal control requirements.

The only potentially relevant aspect is the asynchronous multi-frequency design, where different components run at different rates. Project B already implements this principle with the Planner operating at lower frequency than the Controller and Safety layers. The foundation model integration does not align with Project B's design philosophy of fully learned, end-to-end trainable hierarchies.

## What to Borrow / Implement
- **Style-conditioned single policy** architecture: train one policy that covers multiple locomotion behaviors, conditioned on a style embedding (even without language, use task-specific embeddings)
- **Asynchronous multi-frequency execution** pattern: high-level modules at 2-5 Hz, low-level control at 50+ Hz, with most recent command buffered
- **Three-stage training procedure:** (1) diverse repertoire pre-training, (2) grounding module alignment, (3) end-to-end fine-tuning, applicable to any hierarchical system
- **Teacher-student style training:** use a privileged teacher oracle during simulation training, replace with deployed perception at test time

## Limitations & Open Questions
- **Foundation model compute cost:** running VLM and LLM on a legged robot requires significant onboard compute or offboard communication, limiting deployment scenarios
- **Language ambiguity:** natural language instructions are inherently ambiguous ("walk carefully" means different things on different terrains); the system lacks formal safety guarantees
- **Limited style space:** the 32-64 dimensional style vector may not capture the full diversity of useful locomotion behaviors, particularly for complex terrains or recovery motions
- **Sim-to-real gap for vision:** VLM terrain classification accuracy drops significantly in real-world settings (78% vs 95% in sim), and misclassification can lead to inappropriate gait selection
