# Learning Bipedal Walking on a Quadruped Robot via Adversarial Motion Priors

## Abstract Summary
This paper extends the Adversarial Motion Priors (AMP) framework to teach quadruped robots to walk bipedally using human motion capture data as the adversarial prior. It uses teacher-student policy transfer with a GAN-style discriminator to encourage human-like bipedal gaits on a quadruped platform, demonstrating the flexibility of adversarial motion priors across morphologies.

## Core Contributions
- Extends Adversarial Motion Priors (AMP) to cross-morphology motion transfer (human → quadruped bipedal)
- Demonstrates successful bipedal walking on quadruped hardware using human motion capture references
- Uses a GAN-style discriminator to shape rewards without hand-engineered reward functions
- Employs teacher-student policy transfer for sim-to-real deployment
- Shows that natural human-like gait patterns emerge through adversarial training
- Validates that AMP generalizes beyond same-morphology imitation to cross-embodiment scenarios
- Provides evidence that motion priors can replace complex reward engineering for locomotion

## Methodology Deep-Dive
The Adversarial Motion Priors (AMP) framework replaces hand-crafted reward functions with a learned discriminator that distinguishes between reference motion data and the agent's generated motion. In this work, the reference data comes from human motion capture of bipedal walking, while the agent is a simulated quadruped robot. The key insight is that the discriminator learns to evaluate motion style (bipedal, upright, rhythmic stepping) rather than exact joint-angle matching, enabling cross-morphology transfer.

The training setup follows a GAN-style adversarial process. The policy (generator) produces locomotion behaviors in simulation, while the discriminator is trained to classify state transitions as either "from the reference dataset" or "generated by the policy." The policy receives reward proportional to how well it fools the discriminator—i.e., how closely its generated motion resembles the reference human walking. This adversarial reward signal automatically captures the essential qualities of bipedal walking without requiring explicit reward terms for uprightness, step frequency, or foot clearance.

The state representation for the discriminator is carefully designed to be morphology-agnostic where possible. Rather than comparing raw joint angles (which differ between human and quadruped), the discriminator operates on features like body height, orientation, velocity, and limb phase relationships. This abstraction enables the discriminator to evaluate whether the quadruped's behavior matches the high-level characteristics of human walking without requiring exact kinematic correspondence.

Teacher-student transfer is used for deployment. The teacher policy is trained in simulation with access to privileged information (ground-truth contact states, terrain features), while the student is distilled to use only proprioceptive observations available on the real robot. The AMP reward shaping occurs during teacher training, and the student inherits the bipedal walking behavior through behavior cloning. Domain randomization during both phases ensures robustness to the sim-to-real gap.

The resulting behavior shows the quadruped robot standing on its hind legs and walking in a bipedal fashion with human-like gait characteristics: alternating stance/swing phases, appropriate step frequency, and body sway patterns reminiscent of human walking. This demonstrates that AMP can transfer not just motion style but fundamental locomotion strategies across different body morphologies.

## Key Results & Numbers
- Successful bipedal walking on quadruped robot hardware (real-world deployment)
- Natural human-like gait patterns emerge without explicit gait reward terms
- Cross-morphology motion transfer validated (human motion capture → quadruped bipedal)
- GAN-style discriminator effectively captures locomotion style across different embodiments
- Teacher-student transfer enables real-world deployment from simulation training
- Eliminates the need for hand-engineered bipedal walking reward functions

## Relevance to Project A — Mini Cheetah Quadruped RL
**Rating: Medium**
The AMP cross-morphology transfer demonstrates reward-free gait learning applicable to Mini Cheetah. Instead of hand-engineering reward functions for specific gaits, AMP can learn natural locomotion styles from motion capture references. This could be used to teach Mini Cheetah animal-like gaits (e.g., cat trotting, dog bounding) using animal motion capture data as adversarial priors. The approach simplifies reward design—a major challenge in the PPO training pipeline—by replacing it with reference motion data.

## Limitations & Open Questions
- Cross-morphology transfer quality depends heavily on the choice of morphology-agnostic features for the discriminator
- GAN training instability may lead to mode collapse where the policy converges to a single gait pattern
- Human motion capture may not produce energy-efficient gaits for non-humanoid morphologies
- The discriminator may overfit to specific reference demonstrations rather than capturing general walking characteristics
- Bipedal balance on a quadruped is inherently unstable; robustness to perturbations during bipedal walking is limited
- Scaling to diverse locomotion styles (running, turning, stairs) requires correspondingly diverse reference datasets
